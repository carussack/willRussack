25TH FLOOR

For five months in 2013, I shared a room on the 25th floor of one of the 1,250 skyscrapers that crowd the Hong Kong skyline – the most of any city in the world. As a result, in the most compressed areas Hong Kong has a population density of 91,500 people per square mile. In those areas, it feels as though everyone has a veil over their head, preventing them from seeing the people who constantly surround them. Perhaps that’s why everyone runs into and pushes each other. Pushing, always pushing; onto the bus, into the elevator, into the train – there is no such thing as personal space in Hong Kong.
It is believable that the first time I found myself alone in the city, it was an unconscious response to the suffocating density. As I wandered the streets, searching for these refuges within the city became a sort of mission. There was something surreal and mystical about these spaces, and they provided a brief respite from the crowds and swarms that could be overwhelming.
Yet they never stayed unoccupied for long. Often I invaded someone else’s refuge, and the trace of recent human presence always lingered in the air.
